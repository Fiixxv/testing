— TOME I —

Record of the Veilfall

✦ ✦ ✦

"When the Veil fell, the world did not end. It became a ledger."

The Great Fracture

✦ ✦ ✦

The Veil was not a wall. It was a system — a membrane of compressed consequence separating what mortals did from what that doing cost. Beneath it, the Abyssal Weald existed like a pressure gauge: every act of cruelty, every war, every accumulated atrocity added weight to a ledger no one was watching. The Veil was designed to hold indefinitely, provided the weight remained manageable. The architects of the Veil had, apparently, overestimated humanity's restraint.

When the density of accumulated sin reached its tipping point, the Veil did not tear at its seams. It shattered from the center outward — a single catastrophic implosion that became an explosion in the same instant, sending fracture lines across every part of the sky simultaneously. This was Year Zero. This was the Veilfall. It was not an attack. It was a consequence. Which may be why it has always felt worse than an attack would have.

The sky did not bleed; it broke.
And what descended through the fractures came not as invaders — but as residents, returning to a home they had always occupied in spirit. They had simply been waiting for the door to give way.

The Whispering Monoliths

✦ ✦ ✦

The Monoliths did not appear after the Veil fell. They were always there. The Whispering Monoliths are shards of the Veil's original skeleton — the physical anchors through which the membrane once maintained its shape. When the Veil shattered, the skeleton survived. Seventy-seven fragments of petrified law, buried or exposed depending on the angle of the collapse, scattered across the Overworld. They are exceptionally rare. Many Awakened live and die without ever seeing one.

The Monoliths do not want to be found by the wrong people. Hunters have spent decades searching for a single one, only for a stranger in a ruined city to notice a rock formation that should not exist. The Monolith chooses the moment of encounter. The hunter only chooses what to do next. To stand before a Monolith is to feel the Law you are meant to carry before any words are spoken — as though you were selected in advance, for something specific, by something patient. The Vow is not proposed. It is recognized. And the price is always proportional to the Law's power. The Monolith does not haggle.

The Law does not choose the worthy. It chooses the willing.
Whether those two categories overlap is something the Veil's architects chose not to specify.

The Resonance Debt

✦ ✦ ✦

Before the Veilfall, the cost of power was invisible. A microscopic tithe of self, extracted automatically with each act that bent the world — absorbed into the Veil's membrane with every Vow, every invocation, every moment of deliberate force. A blink of a memory. The texture of a particular sound. A color perceived slightly differently, once, and never recovered. Imperceptible losses. The Veil managed this quietly, gracefully, in amounts too small to grieve. When the Veil shattered, the collection mechanism broke. The ledger did not.

This is the Resonance Debt. Every activation of a Sacred Vow, every Pyre Binding, every act of Reaping crystallizes a fragment — a piece of the practitioner made physical and taken. Not always a memory. Sometimes a concept. The capacity for doubt. The name of a particular color. The shape of a specific laugh. The word for "enough." The fragments float free and dissolve into the Veil's residue. You are never told what will be taken. You are never told when.

The Archive does not fear the Debt. They have classified it as a solvable equation, a system to be reverse-engineered and ultimately broken. Their senior scholars above Rank A operate with obvious gaps — voids in their personhood that you notice only after minutes of conversation, like a sentence missing its subject. They seem unbothered by this. The Archive believes it is a temporary condition on the road to something larger. The Archive is frequently wrong in ways that cost other people dearly.

The Inquisition's answer to the Debt is the only rigorous institutional response anyone has produced. They log every use of power. They deliberately offer controlled sacrifices during each Binding — small, chosen things — to prevent the Debt from taking something unplanned. Inquisitors who have kept perfect records for decades are among the most functionally complete people left in the world. They have simply chosen, deliberately and repeatedly, what to give away. There is an argument that this is wisdom. There is an equally strong argument that choosing your own erosion is still erosion.

The Veilborn: Sin-Eaters

✦ ✦ ✦

The Veilborn are not invaders. This is the most important thing the Archive eventually documented, and the most uncomfortable thing the Inquisition refuses to teach. The Veilborn were created by the Overworld. Every act of cruelty committed in the light was absorbed into the Abyssal Weald, where it became something that breathed. The Weald is not another dimension — it is a mirror, containing in monstrous form everything humanity refused to look at directly. The Sin-Eaters are that refusal, given tendrils and teeth and a specific kind of patience.

They do not simply rampage. They evolve — progressing through seven evolutionary phases tied to the Seven Apostasies, each one fueled by a corresponding human failing. A freshly emerged Veilborn is pure reflex. A Phase IV entity is compulsive and catastrophic. A Phase VI is singular and devoted in ways that are almost indistinguishable from obsession. Phase VII is theorized. No recorded Phase VII entity has been destroyed and documented. The Archive's models suggest such an entity would cease to resemble a creature and begin to resemble a condition — something closer to weather than to war.

The Veilborn do not seek to destroy the Whispering Monoliths because they hate them. They seek to destroy them because the Monoliths represent a principle they cannot metabolize: that power has a price. The Ravaging is not conquest. It is a philosophical argument, conducted in architecture.

The Awakened: The Reapers

✦ ✦ ✦

The first Reaping was not a technique. It was an accident. A hunter in the ruins of Durvast — the first city swallowed by a Sin-Eater — knelt over the dissolving corpse of the Veilborn that had killed her partner. The golden residue of its death seeped into her hands before she could move away. She survived the night. Every hunter who had stood near Veilborn deaths and lived through it had been doing the same thing for months without knowing what it meant. The Archive was simply the first to name the mechanism.

The Reaping is the act of drawing Astral Essence — the residue of a Veilborn's dissolution — into one's own body. The body knows how to do it. What it does not know how to do is stop. Each Reaping makes a hunter stronger and draws them incrementally further from ordinary human experience. The Awakened walk a line between advancement and dissolution, knowing that the same process which makes them capable of fighting the Veilborn makes them progressively more difficult to distinguish from what they are fighting.

We are not heroes. We are the predictable outcome of desperate people in contact with something they should not survive. Every Reaping changes the math — more of what kills the Veilborn, less of what makes us worth saving. We hunt to protect the living. But we protect the living by becoming progressively less like them.

The Iron Inquisition

✦ ✦ ✦

The Inquisition did not begin as an institution. It began as a question: if the Veilborn are made from human sin, and the Awakened grow stronger by absorbing Veilborn essence, then at what point does a Rank-S Reaper become functionally indistinguishable from the thing they hunt? The first Inquisitors had no answer. They simply decided the question was worth enforcing at swordpoint.

The Pyre Covenant was developed not as a path to power but as a path to accountability. The burning of Veilborn fragments and Monolith shards is secondary to the ritual's true function: it forces a record. Every Binding is logged, witnessed, categorized. The power extracted is known. The price paid is chosen rather than taken. An Inquisitor who has performed thirty Bindings can tell you precisely what they have surrendered, in what order, for what return. The Archive considers this obsessive. The Inquisition considers it the only remaining proof of humanity in a person who has deliberately weaponized themselves.

The Inquisition does not hunt monsters. It hunts thresholds. The question is never "what has this person done?" The question is always "how much of themselves remains?" When the answer falls below acceptable tolerance, the Inquisition acts. They are correct often enough that the Archive tolerates them. They are wrong often enough that the Archive hates them.

The Inquisition's unspoken position on the Veilfall is singular: they believe the membrane fell not because of the Veilborn, but because of what humanity was already becoming. Not their acts — their trajectory. The decision to absorb power rather than contain it, to grow rather than resist, is what made the Veil's accumulated weight unmanageable. They cannot prove this. They operate as though it were already proven.

The Great Conflict

✦ ✦ ✦

Three forces. Three answers to the same question: what do you do when the world breaks? The Archive's answer is ascension — claim every remaining Monolith through Sacred Vows before the Veilborn can shatter them, rebuild the membrane with the Awakened as its new framework rather than its residents. This requires hunters willing to accept Vows whose prices have driven previous candidates into silence, into madness, or into something that no longer answers to its own name. The Archive considers these acceptable casualties in a war of arithmetic.

The Inquisition's answer is control — regulate power, keep the Awakened below the threshold where they become threats equivalent to the Veilborn, and let the broken world persist indefinitely rather than risk a second fracture wearing a hopeful face. Their calculus is cold. It is also, by certain metrics, correct. The Veilborn have no answer. They have an appetite. And an appetite is considerably harder to argue with than a philosophy.

There is an argument, made quietly and never published, that the three-way stalemate is the Veil's remaining equilibrium — that as long as no single force wins, the remaining Monoliths survive, and the Convergence is held indefinitely at bay. The person who first made this argument subsequently lost their ability to explain why it mattered. The Archive archived the document anyway.

The Seven Apostasies

✦ ✦ ✦

— Archive Classification Document · Filed under: Veilborn Evolutionary Biology · Access: Rank B and above —

A Veilborn is born empty. Not of ambition or capacity — Phase I Veilborn are already more lethal than most Rank-B Awakened — but empty of direction. It is a hunger without a palate. What transforms a Veilborn from a mindless force into something that begins to make decisions is the absorption of human emotional residue in sufficient density. The Archive calls these evolutionary stages the Seven Apostasies, after the seven moral failures they correspond to. The naming was deliberate: you cannot study what you refuse to acknowledge as familiar.

PHASE I — WRATH Pure reflex. Destruction as the primary expression of existence. Phase I Veilborn cannot distinguish between targets — everything in proximity is engaged. They do not retreat. They are physiologically incapable of retreat, because retreat requires intention, and intention requires a self. Roughly 60% of all active Veilborn never evolve past this phase. The Archive considers this fortunate. The Archive is correct to consider this fortunate.
PHASE II — PRIDE The Veilborn begins selecting targets. It develops a preference for prey that resists — that fights back, that has something worth breaking. Phase II entities will bypass weak targets entirely to reach a stronger opponent. They have begun to understand hierarchy. They are simply placing themselves at its top by force. This is the phase at which the Inquisition classifies them as "sentient threats" rather than "natural disasters."
PHASE III — ENVY The Veilborn becomes aware of what others possess. Phase III entities have been observed watching Awakened for extended periods before engaging — studying movement patterns, Vow-power deployment, social structures. They want what the Awakened have. Since they cannot obtain it through exchange, they obtain it through consumption. This is when Veilborn first begin targeting Monoliths directly, rather than as incidental terrain.
PHASE IV — GLUTTONY Consumption becomes compulsive. The Veilborn in this phase no longer simply hunts — it absorbs everything within reach, including things it cannot metabolize or use. The Ravaging of Monoliths becomes catastrophic in this phase, not from strategic calculation but because a Phase IV entity cannot remain near a source of power without consuming it. Xyron reached Phase IV at a documented rate three times faster than average. The Archive flagged this and immediately lost the file.
PHASE V — SLOTH Counter-intuitively, the most dangerous phase of early evolution. The Veilborn becomes still. It has fed enough to develop patience — and patience in a Veilborn is more destabilizing than rage. Phase V entities select positions, establish territories, and wait. A Phase V Veilborn watching a settlement for weeks before moving is not resting. It is preparing with a precision its previous phases did not have the capacity to access.
PHASE VI — LUST Singular obsession crystallizes. The Veilborn in this phase has identified something it wants with a specificity that is, uncomfortably, indistinguishable from devotion. A Phase VI entity will pursue one target — a specific hunter, a specific Monolith, a specific city — with total exclusion of all other objectives. Other Veilborn, Inquisitors, Archive battalions: all are obstacles to be cleared between it and what it has decided to own. No Phase VI Veilborn has ever been successfully redirected.
PHASE VII — GREED Theoretical. No confirmed Phase VII Veilborn has been destroyed and recorded. The Archive's models suggest a Phase VII entity would cease to resemble an organism and begin to resemble a condition — something closer to a geological event than a creature. Identification criteria have been drafted. The field teams assigned to draft them did not file subsequent reports. The Archive has left the document open. They have not assigned new teams.

The Recorded Debts

✦ ✦ ✦

— Inquisition Registry of Verified Resonance Debt Losses · Open Archive · Updated continuously —

The Resonance Debt does not always take what you would call important. This appears to be intentional. The following entries represent a fraction of the 1,847 verified Debt losses currently on record among living registered practitioners. 214 are classified as operationally significant. 12 are classified as "of unknown consequence." The remaining 1,621 are classified as manageable. Manageable is not the same as fine. The Archive has noted this distinction in the file, and then continued.

Archivist Mireille Chen · Rank A · Loss #114 on Record Lost the concept of "indoors." Not the memory of being inside buildings — the sensation of enclosure itself. She understands, intellectually, that she is inside a room. She does not experience it as such. She sleeps in open fields by preference. The Archive has classified this as functionally negligible. She has submitted six reassignment requests and does not remember submitting any of them.
Reaper Yusuf, "The Ironwood" · Rank S · Loss #3 of an Unknown Total Lost the ability to feel warmth. Not the pain of burning — the warmth of things. Sunlight does not register as warm. Other people, when he touches them, feel like objects at room temperature. He has not altered his field behavior in any measurable way. His chronicle entry notes that this was the third thing taken. He does not know what the first two were. He has stopped trying to identify them.
Grand Inquisitor Severin Cole · Rank S · Unlogged Bindings, Estimated 8 Lost every memory of every conversation he has ever had about fear. Not the capacity to feel fear — the conversations. He knows fear is a concept. He knows he once discussed it. He cannot reconstruct the texture of a single exchange. The Inquisition's official position is that this was an acceptable consequence of eight unlogged Bindings during the Battle of Ashenfeld West. Grand Inquisitor Cole's position is that he has forgotten what his position used to be and is working from first principles. He considers this adequate.
Hunter-Archivist Noa Voss · Rank A · Loss #8 on Record Lost the word "almost." Not the concept of approximation — the word. When she tries to express something close to an outcome but not quite the outcome, she cannot find the language for the middle distance. She says "it happened" or "it did not happen." She has been reassigned to strategic assessment, where her colleagues find her unusually decisive. She has not told them why.
Unnamed · Rank B · Entry submitted by field partner · Unverified Lost the ability to recognize when a conversation is over. Continues speaking past natural endpoints. Does not register silence as a response. Field partner reports that debriefs now take three to four times longer than necessary. Partner requests reassignment. Reassignment denied. The Inquisition considers the loss non-operationally-significant. The field partner's disagreement has been noted.

The Archive continues to track these entries under the working thesis that Debt losses follow a pattern — that the world is not taking randomly but selectively, removing specific categories of experience in a sequence that has not yet resolved into a legible sentence. They have not published this thesis. Several of the researchers assigned to it have subsequently lost the ability to explain why they found it important. The documents remain open.
